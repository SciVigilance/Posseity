posseity

[ poh-sey-ih-tee ]  ·  IPA /poʊˈseɪɪti/

noun; plural posseities (sense 1)

Some things are invented, we say, and some are discovered. But there is a case for saying that nothing is ever truly invented, only discovered, that anything which can exist in the universe is already a real possibility, waiting, whether or not anyone ever reaches it. Powered flight was possible throughout human history. The Wright brothers did not create that possibility. They arrived at it.

If that is right, then a possibility is not merely a thought or a hypothetical. It is a real, fixed feature of what the universe permits, as definite before it is actualized as after. English has words for the possible object (possibilia), for the space possibilities occupy (possibility space), and for positions one can hold about them (possibilism, modal realism). It has no single word for the plain fact that a possibility is real as a possibility, in itself, independent of whether it is ever built or found.

Posseity is a word for exactly that.

Definition

1

the property of a possibility being a real, fixed, and definite part of what the universe permits, fully itself whether or not it is ever actualized; the reality of the possibility itself, as distinct from the object that might fill it and from the space it occupies.

2

the entire domain of such possibilities taken together; the complete, fixed field of everything the universe permits to exist, whether known or unknown, discovered or not, actualized or not.

The two senses share one root in the manner of humanity, which denotes both a person's humanity (the quality) and humanity as a whole (all people). Context determines which is meant. The plural, posseities, refers to several individual possibilities considered together, though not the whole field.

The Core Idea

Begin with a single claim: the set of things the universe permits is fixed. Not fixed by anyone, not decided, simply a fact about how reality is built. A given arrangement of matter is either allowed by the laws of the universe or it is not. If it is allowed, then it is a genuine possibility, and it was a genuine possibility before anyone thought of it, and it will remain one whether or not anyone ever builds it.

Posseity is the name for the reality that such a possibility already has. Not the reality of the built thing. The reality of the possibility. A specific object, made of specific atoms arranged a specific way, is a permitted configuration of the universe. That permission is not nothing. It is a real, determinate fact, as settled before the object exists as after. When someone finally builds the object, they do not grant it permission to be possible. The permission was always there. They only move it from possible to actual.

This is why, on this view, the language of invention is slightly misleading. To invent is not to bring a possibility into being; the possibility was already there, holding its posseity. To invent is to locate a possibility and actualize it. The inventor is a kind of discoverer, and what they discover is a region of the fixed field of the permitted. The bearing, the melody, the equation, the machine: each held posseity long before any mind reached it.

Two Registers of the Real

The heart of the concept is a distinction that ordinary language blurs. When we say something is "real," we usually mean it is actual, present in the world at some place and time. But a possibility is real in a different way. It is real as a possibility. These are two registers of reality, not two amounts of it.

This matters because it resists a natural mistake, the idea that a possibility is a faint or partial version of the actual thing, a shadow waiting to be filled in. It is not. A possibility has its full reality already, complete and exact, as a possibility. Building the thing does not make its posseity stronger. Posseity does not come in degrees. A possibility either belongs to the set of what the universe permits or it does not. There is no halfway. The melody nobody has played holds its posseity as completely as the melody played a thousand times. Actualization adds an actual instance to the world; it adds nothing to the possibility's standing as possible.

So a thing may hold full posseity and never be actualized. Most things do. The overwhelming majority of what the universe permits will never be built, never be found, never be thought of. Their posseity is undiminished by this neglect. They are no less real as possibilities for never becoming actual.

The Field of Posseity

Sense 2 gathers all of it together. Take every possibility that holds posseity, every permitted configuration of the universe, known and unknown, and consider them as one. That total is the field of posseity: the complete, fixed catalog of everything the universe allows to exist.

It is invisible and mostly undiscovered. We illuminate small regions of it over time. Each discovery, each invention, each cure and machine and word, is one more point in the field lit up and made actual. Nothing is ever added to the field, because the field was always complete. Discovery does not enlarge what is possible; it reveals more of what was already permitted. In this sense every act of making is an act of finding.

The field has a structure worth noticing. Some possibilities sit in isolation, reachable with no prerequisites, waiting only for someone to notice them. Others cluster: once one is discovered, the things it makes reachable become apparent, and a discovery can serve as a stepping stone to further discoveries nearby. There is no single starting point and no fixed order. It is less a ladder than a vast puzzle whose whole picture already exists before any piece is placed, some pieces standing alone, some densely connected to their neighbours. What is discovered can aid the discovery of what is adjacent, but nothing in the field depends on being discovered in order to be real. It was all real, as posseity, from the start.

Etymology

From Latin posse ("to be able, to be possible") plus -ity ("state or quality of"), on the model of quiddity ("the whatness of a thing," from Latin quid, "what"). Just as quiddity names what a thing is, posseity names the reality a thing holds as merely possible.

In scholastic Latin, posse itself served as a noun meaning potentiality, the capability of being. The root has older kin in the fifteenth-century coinages of Nicholas of Cusa, who fused posse with est ("is") to make possest, and later proposed posse ipsum ("ability itself"), both as names for God, the union of possibility and actuality. Posseity shares that root but points elsewhere: not at a divine union of the possible and the actual, but at the plain, fixed reality that any ordinary possibility holds in itself.

The pronunciation, poh-sey-ih-tee, is deliberate: the first syllable echoes "possibility," the thing the word is about.

Neighbours and Distinctions

Posseity sits among several established ideas without duplicating any of them. Setting it beside each one sharpens what it means.

Against potentiality

Aristotle's potentiality (Greek dynamis) is the capacity of a particular thing to become something else: the acorn's potential to be an oak, the block of marble's potential to be a statue. Potentiality lives in an existing thing and points forward to what it could become. Posseity is not lodged in any existing thing. It is the reality of the possibility itself, whether or not anything now exists that could bring it about. The statue's posseity does not wait on the marble; it is a permitted configuration of the universe regardless.

Against possibilia

Possibilia are possible objects, the possible things themselves, considered as items: the golden mountain, the person who was never born. Posseity is not the possible object. It is the property that object has of being genuinely permitted, or the whole field of such permissions. Possibilia are what fill the slots; posseity is the reality of the slots.

Against possibility space

Possibility space (or, in physics, configuration space or state space) is the space in which possibilities are arrayed, the coordinate system of the possible. Posseity is not the space. It is the reality of what the space contains, taken as a fixed and determinate totality rather than as a mere range of coordinates.

Against possibilism and modal realism

Possibilism is the position that possibility is an irreducible feature of reality. Modal realism, associated with David Lewis, is the stronger view that possibilities are as real as actualities, that other possible worlds exist as concretely as our own. These are positions one can hold. Posseity is not a position; it is the thing the positions are about. A possibilist affirms that possibilities have posseity. A modal realist affirms that their posseity is as full as actuality's. One can name posseity without committing to either view about how much reality it ultimately has.

Against the adjacent possible

Stuart Kauffman's adjacent possible describes what reality makes reachable in one step from what currently exists, the next moves available from here. It is dynamic and indexed to the present state of the world. Posseity is static and total. The adjacent possible is a moving frontier within the field of posseity; the field itself does not move, does not grow, and does not depend on where the frontier happens to be.

Why the Word Is Needed

Each neighbour above names something adjacent, and each leaves a gap in the same place. We can name the possible object, the space it sits in, and the stances we take toward possibility. We have no plain, single word for the property a possibility has of being real as a possibility, in itself, fixed and definite, prior to and independent of anyone reaching it.

The gap is easy to demonstrate. Ask what powered flight had, throughout all the centuries before anyone flew, that made it a genuine possibility rather than a mere fancy. It had something. It was permitted by the universe; a flying machine was a real configuration reality allowed, unlike a square circle, which is not. That "something" it had is what this word names. Before now, to point at it one had to reach for a phrase, "its status as a genuine unactualized possibility", or borrow a nearby term and stretch it. Posseity gives the thing its own name.

Naming it is not a small thing. A concept without a word must be reconstructed from scratch each time it is needed, and so it tends not to be thought at all. A word makes the idea portable: it can be carried into a sentence, set beside other ideas, argued over, refined. Whether the concept earns a lasting place is a question only use can answer. But the word now exists for anyone who reaches the same gap and needs a handle for it.

In a Sentence

"Powered flight held posseity throughout human history; the Wright brothers did not create the possibility, they reached it."
"A melody no one has ever played still holds posseity, complete and waiting."
"To discover something is to recognize its posseity; to build it is to actualize what already held posseity."
"Nothing is ever added to posseity; discovery only lights up more of the field."
"Somewhere within posseity lies every cure not yet found."

Related Terms

possibilia — possible objects, considered as things rather than as a property.

possibility space — the space possibilities occupy, rather than their reality.

possibilism — the position that possibility is an irreducible feature of reality; one who holds it affirms posseity.

modal realism — the view that possibilities are as real as actualities.

adjacent possible — what reality makes reachable one step out from what already exists.

quiddity — the whatness of a thing; the model on which posseity is formed.
