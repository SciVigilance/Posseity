A Coined Word

posseity

[ poe-sey-ih-tee ]  ·  IPA /poʊˈseɪɪti/

noun; plural posseities (sense 1)

Some things are invented, we say, and some are discovered. But there is a case for saying that nothing is ever truly invented, only discovered, that anything which can exist in the universe is already a real possibility, waiting, whether or not anyone ever reaches it. Powered flight was possible throughout human history. The Wright brothers did not create that possibility. They arrived at it.

If that is right, then a possibility is not merely a thought or a hypothetical. It is a real, fixed feature of what the universe permits, as definite before it is actualized as after. English has words for the possible object (possibilia), for the space possibilities occupy (possibility space), and for positions one can hold about them (possibilism, modal realism). It has no single word for the plain fact that a possibility is real as a possibility, in itself, independent of whether it is ever built or found.

Posseity is a word for exactly that.

Definition

1

the property of a possibility being a real, fixed, and definite part of what the universe permits, fully itself whether or not it is ever actualized; the reality of the possibility itself, as distinct from the object that might fill it and from the space it occupies.

2

the entire domain of such possibilities taken together; the complete, fixed field of everything the universe permits to exist, whether known or unknown, discovered or not, actualized or not.

The two senses share one root in the manner of humanity, which denotes both a person's humanity (the quality) and humanity as a whole (all people). Context determines which is meant.

Etymology

From Latin posse ("to be able, to be possible") plus -ity ("state or quality of"), on the model of quiddity ("the whatness of a thing"). In scholastic Latin, posse itself served as a noun meaning potentiality, the capability of being. The root has older kin in Nicholas of Cusa's fifteenth-century coinages possest and posse ipsum; related in ancestry, distinct in meaning.

The Distinction

A posseity is real as a possibility but not real as an actuality. These are two different registers of "real," not two points upon one scale. A thing may hold full posseity and never be actualized, and actualization does not increase its posseity. It only builds it.

To discover something, on this view, is to recognize its posseity. To make it is to actualize what already held posseity. Nothing is added to what is possible; discovery only lights up more of the field.

In a Sentence

"A melody no one has ever played still holds posseity, complete and waiting."
"Every invention is a discovery drawn out of posseity. We do not add to what is possible; we illuminate part of it."
"Somewhere within posseity lies every cure not yet found."

Related Terms

possibilia — possible objects, considered as things rather than as a property.

possibility space — the space possibilities occupy, rather than their reality.

possibilism — the philosophical position that possibility is an irreducible feature of reality; one who holds it affirms posseity.

modal realism — the view that possibilities are as real as actualities.
